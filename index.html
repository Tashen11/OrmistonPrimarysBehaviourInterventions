Anxiety

  • Worry about everyday things and are unable to control them.
  • Frequently ask for help with tasks they can do independently.
  • Apprehensive to start and work independently, needing constant reassurance once they begin.
  • Seem unsure of themselves with low self-confidence and self-esteem.
  • Do not smile often, appear unable to relax, are easily startled, and have difficulty concentrating.
  • Assure the learner that everyone experiences stress, frustration, worry, or feeling overwhelmed.
  • Validate their experiences with statements like: “I know you are frustrated because you feel this assignment is too hard.”
  • Normalize the feeling: “I also get frustrated when I think something is hard, and so do other learners.”
  • Teach practical relaxation techniques: deep breathing, counting to 10, or journaling.
  • Scaffold tasks by breaking them into smaller parts and allowing breaks after each part is completed.
  • Teach coping skills: recognize physical signs (sweaty, racing heart), label the emotion, and use strategies to regulate.
  • Provide proximal seating and check on them often.
  • Use reflective listening: repeat back what the learner says to show understanding.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss concerns with the Senior Leadership Team (SLT).
  • Meet with parents to discuss concerns and strategies.
  • Encourage parents to seek a GP appointment if they have concerns about anxiety.
  • Consider external referrals to health or social services.
  • Provide counseling support, either private or through GP sessions.
  • Offer one-on-one mentoring.
  • Implement check-in/check-out procedures, where learners check in daily with an adult for encouragement.
  • Use non-verbal cues and signals to communicate when feeling overwhelmed.
  • Provide sensory tools and toys to assist in managing anxiety.

Defiance

  • Lash out verbally at others and engage in arguments and conflict.
  • Unwilling to let issues go or drop them.
  • Dislike being told what to do and often do the opposite of what is instructed.
  • Exhibit physical signs of defiance, such as crossing arms or stomping feet, when asked to follow directives.
  • Become aggressive and act out when asked to do something.
  • Refuse to comply with adult requests and follow classroom routines.
  • Only engage in tasks or activities they like, displaying tantrums or meltdowns when asked to do something else.
  • Acknowledge positive behavior to encourage compliance.
  • Avoid power struggles by remaining calm and stating expectations clearly.
  • Provide clear and concise directions using a neutral tone.
  • Offer acceptable choices and allow the learner to decide within 10 seconds or you choose for them.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss concerns with the Senior Leadership Team (SLT) or redirect the learner to SLT.
  • Engage with parents through hui (meetings).
  • Implement time out of the classroom by redirecting to the office.
  • Offer time out during iBreak as a cooling-off period.
  • Set goals, monitor progress, and use a daily tracking sheet.
  • Establish a behavior contract with clear expectations.

Aggression/Bullying

  • Verbally or physically harass others.
  • Engage in bullying activities, intimidation, and threats.
  • Be observed hitting, kicking, or repeatedly pushing others.
  • Demonstrate intense anger.
  • Frequently lose temper or have explosive outbursts.
  • Hurt or interact roughly with others during play, recess, or free time.
  • Name-calling and derogatory remarks.
  • Instigate and engage in frequent conflicts, arguments, and fights.
  • Don't overreact; maintain control of the situation.
  • Redirect behavior by reinforcing established rules and expectations.
  • Minimize verbal instructions; utilize non-verbal signals and choices to redirect behavior.
  • Use the Zones of Regulation toolkit for strategies.
  • Clearly define and consistently enforce rules regarding respectful behavior, bullying, and aggression, making sure the learner understands the consequences.
  • Intervene early to prevent escalation and convey that such actions are unacceptable.
  • Utilize restorative practices such as group discussions and reflection on actions.
  • Encourage open communication; create a safe environment for students to express their feelings and concerns.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with Senior Leadership Team (SLT) or redirect the learner to SLT.
  • Engage parents through hui (meetings).
  • Implement time out of the classroom environment.
  • Use time out during iBreaks.
  • Gradually reintroduce the learner back into the classroom or playground.
  • Conduct restorative conversations to address behavior.
  • Maintain daily behavior tracking to monitor progress.
  • Create a Behavior Intervention Plan with support from SLT.

Disrespectful/Confrontational

  • Frequent confrontation with adults and peers.
  • Regularly talking back to adults.
  • Destruction of others’ property.
  • Taking and using others’ belongings without permission.
  • Dismissive behavior toward others.
  • Unwillingness to consider others’ ideas and opinions.
  • Disregard for others’ feelings, well-being, and safety.
  • Verbal lashing out at others.
  • Challenging the authority of adults.
  • Refusal to comply with adult requests.
  • Ignoring habitat routines.
  • Acknowledge positive behavior. Recognize when the learner behaves appropriately, pays attention, stays on task, is prepared for work, sits quietly, or shows kindness.
  • Avoid power struggles; remain calm.
  • Redirect behavior by asking the learner to find a space to calm down and take a break.
  • Explain assignments/directions using eye contact; have the learner repeat directions back to you.
  • Implement consequences that match the severity of the behavior, such as missing iBreak or withdrawing privileges.
  • Walk away from the situation; do not engage in a conversation about it.
  • Encourage the learner to reflect on their behavior using the Reflection sheet.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss the situation with the Senior Leadership Team (SLT) or redirect the learner to SLT.
  • Conduct hui (meetings) with parents.
  • Implement time out of the classroom; redirect the learner to the office.
  • Conduct restorative conversations to address behavior.

Off-task/Disruptive

  • Annoying and distracting to others.
  • Pestering peers.
  • Asking many obvious questions.
  • Making frequent and unnecessary comments.
  • Engaging in tasks other than assigned work.
  • Talking to others frequently.
  • Throwing objects.
  • Yelling out in class.
  • Avoid power struggles; remain calm, cool, and collected.
  • Clearly explain assignments and directions.
  • Simplify tasks as needed.
  • Use eye contact and engage at the learner's level when explaining assignments.
  • Have the learner repeat and explain each part of the directions back to you.
  • Encourage moving to a new location or teaming up with a buddy.
  • Praise the learner when they are on task. Make praises specific and individualized (e.g., “I like how you brought your folder/work and are ready for learning today.”).
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss the situation with the Senior Leadership Team (SLT) or redirect the learner to SLT.
  • Conduct hui (meetings) with parents.
  • Implement restorative conversations to address behavior.
  • Provide time out of habitat to complete the task.
  • Allow time out of iBreak to complete the task.

Upset/Crying

  • Have frequent emotional meltdowns.
  • Cry frequently over “small” things like lost place in line or wrong answer.
  • Appear distraught, disturbed, or sad.
  • Have trouble pulling themselves together and calming down.
  • Refuse to be comforted, push others away.
  • Be unable to talk or verbally express concerns.
  • Rock, hold head or face in hands, turn away or back to others, put hand up, shake head “no.”
  • Not knowing what they want when asked.
  • Ask to be left alone, run away, or sit in a corner in the habitat.
  • Put hand to mouth and flutter hands and/or arms.
  • Make statements like “I can’t do this,” “This isn’t happening,” “I can’t take this,” etc.
  • Provide reassurance and encourage deep breathing.
  • Speak in a calm, neutral voice. Avoid matching the emotional state of the student.
  • Redirect to strategies from the Zones of Regulation toolkit.
  • Move to a breakout room or another space in the habitat.
  • Instill confidence: “I know you can do it and I think you know you can too.”
  • Allow time for the student to calm down. Offer choices when they are ready.
  • Teach coping skills at a separate time to prepare for future difficult situations.
  • Use reflective listening: repeat back what the learner says to show understanding.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Discuss with SLT (Senior Leadership Team) or redirect the learner to SLT.
  • Hui (meeting) with parents to understand triggers.
  • Time out of the habitat if needed.
  • Perform a well-being check regularly for emotional stability.

Not Ready to Learn

  • Appears confused and distracted.
  • Lacks focus on the task.
  • Shows a lack of engagement with the task.
  • Quietly blends in while doing nothing, doodling, or pretending to work.
  • Spends excessive time searching through things (desk, locker, etc.).
  • Claims to be working on something but produces few results.
  • Does not have necessary tools for the lesson (e.g., books, writing tools).
  • Lacks confidence or has difficulty with comprehension and memory skills.
  • Struggles to follow verbal instructions.
  • Has difficulty following complex instructions.
  • Has poor organizational skills.
  • Makes frequent excuses to avoid tasks.
  • Assign a buddy to assist with transitions, setup, and organization.
  • Break down assignments and simplify instructions. Allow the student to start with easy or hard items, based on their preference.
  • Provide opportunities for the learner to check back after completing small sections of the task.
  • Offer 1:1 support at the start, making eye contact and ensuring attention.
  • Redirect to strategies from the Zones of Regulation toolkit to help prepare the learner for engagement.
  • Use a visual planner or schedule for activities and tasks, including group assignments and required materials.
  • Clearly explain the assignment and directions, making eye contact. Have the learner repeat directions back to you.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Collaborate with SLT or redirect the learner to SLT.
  • Hui (meeting) with parents to discuss potential barriers and support.
  • Offer time out of the habitat to complete the task.
  • Allow time out of iBreak to complete unfinished tasks.
  • Set goals, monitor progress, and track achievement.

Lack of Social Skills

  • Interacts and navigates their environment in an awkward or odd manner.
  • Antagonizes others by annoying, poking, hitting, or seeking attention and then running away.
  • Has poor understanding of appropriate times and places to say certain things.
  • Frequently teases others.
  • Has younger or more immature friends.
  • Displays emotional immaturity.
  • Struggles with making or maintaining friendships.
  • Displays inappropriate behaviors.
  • Always tries to top others, win, or be better than everyone else.
  • Is a poor winner or loser.
  • Plays alone or engages in parallel play.
  • Acts silly to make others laugh.
  • Is overprotective of relationships.
  • Assign a buddy to model appropriate social interactions.
  • Provide specific opportunities for social interaction and teach social skills.
  • Teach appropriate ways to interact with others.
  • Move the learner to a new location to manage behavior.
  • Focus attention on the learner without highlighting their behavior negatively.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care.
  • Collaborate with SLT or redirect the learner to SLT for support.
  • Conduct a hui (meeting) with parents to address concerns.
  • Involve RTLB (Resource Teacher Learning and Behavior) for targeted interventions.
  • Implement a social skills intervention program.
  • Set goals, monitor progress, and track the learner's achievements.

Tantrums/Out of Control

  • Fails to respond to redirection, calming, or de-escalation efforts.
  • Displays extreme oppositional and defiant behavior.
  • Deliberately destroys items, furniture, toys, or supplies.
  • Engages in physical outbursts like yelling, hitting, kicking, biting, flailing, or rolling on the ground.
  • Lays on the floor and refuses to follow instructions.
  • Runs around the room or out of school.
  • Shows no concern for consequences or adult authority.
  • Shouts defiant phrases like “No”, “I don’t want to”, or “Make me.”
  • Avoid power struggles by keeping responses brief and to the point.
  • Use a calm, neutral tone without matching the student’s emotional level.
  • State the expectation and consequence, give the student a choice, and walk away.
  • If the student is too distressed, maintain distance and avoid eye contact.
  • Offer choices and allow time for the student to make a decision, then step away.
  • Use redirection if the learner is able to be de-escalated.
  • Remove the student from the situation and discuss the issue after they have calmed down.
  • Use reflective listening (e.g., “I hear you’re feeling upset”).
  • Count to 10 and redirect the learner to Zones of Regulation if receptive.
  • Move to a quieter corner or a breakout room for calming down.
  • Seek advice from pastoral care or school counselors.
  • Consult with SLT or redirect the learner to SLT for additional support.
  • Hui (meet) with parents to discuss concerns and strategies.
  • Refer to RTLB if behavior persists after Tier 1 strategies have been implemented.
  • Conduct a well-being check to rule out other triggers for emotional outbursts.
  • Create a behavior de-escalation plan in consultation with SLT.
